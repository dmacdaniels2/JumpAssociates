Lithium Battery Lifecycle Analysis
Phase 1: Mining (DRC Cobalt Extraction)

Social Science Perspective

Environmental Injustice Identified:

  • 73% live on $1.90/day (extreme poverty)
  • 40,000 children in dangerous mining
  • Communities of color bear disproportionate burden
  • 150,000+ artisanal miners exploited
  • Economic Inequity: Miners receive pennies while tech companies profit billions

Design Thinking Analysis

  • Clarify: Miners face life-threatening conditions for survival wages
  • Ideate: How might we ensure safe, fair mining practices?
  • Develop: Test safety protocols, wage standards, child protection
  • Implement: Legalized Artisanal and small-scale mining (ASM) with proper oversight

Governmental Strategy

Current Challenge: DRC government economically dependent on mining (⅓ of GDP)

Strategic Options:

  • Regulate rather than ban ASM mining
  • Implement safety standards & worker rights
  • Create economic diversification programs
  • Partner with international organizations
Phase 2: Battery Production & Manufacturing

Social Science Perspective

Global Supply Chain Injustice:

  • Value extraction: Raw materials cheap, finished products expensive
  • Geographic inequality: Harm in Global South, benefits in Global North
  • Corporate responsibility gap in supply chain transparency

Design Thinking Analysis

  • Clarify: Companies like Tesla, Apple need ethical sourcing
  • Ideate: How might we create transparent, ethical supply chains?
  • Develop: Blockchain tracking, ethical certification systems
  • Implement: Fair Cobalt Alliance partnerships

Governmental Strategy

Regulatory Opportunities:

  • Mandatory supply chain disclosure laws
  • Import restrictions on unethically sourced materials
  • International agreements on mining standards
  • Corporate accountability legislation
Phase 3: Consumer Use (EVs & Electronics)

Social Science Perspective

Paradox of Progress:

  • Environmental benefits (reduced CO₂) vs. social harm
  • Consumer awareness gap about supply chain impacts
  • Climate justice vs. economic justice tension

Design Thinking Analysis

  • Clarify: Consumers want both environmental AND ethical products
  • Ideate: How might we enable conscious consumption?
  • Develop: Ethical labeling systems, alternative materials
  • Implement: Consumer education + industry pressure

Governmental Strategy

Policy Balance:

  • Maintain EV incentives while pushing ethical sourcing
  • Consumer right-to-know legislation
  • Research funding for cobalt alternatives
  • Green procurement standards for government
Phase 4: End-of-Life & Disposal

Social Science Perspective

Emerging Justice Concerns:

  • Electronic waste disproportionately affects developing nations
  • Battery recycling facilities often in marginalized communities
  • Long-term environmental impacts still unknown

Design Thinking Analysis

  • Clarify: Current disposal methods may create new problems
  • Ideate: How might we create circular economy for batteries?
  • Develop: Recycling technology, design for disassembly
  • Implement: Closed-loop manufacturing systems

Governmental Strategy

Future-Proofing Policies:

  • Extended producer responsibility laws
  • Battery recycling infrastructure investment
  • International e-waste prevention treaties
  • Circular economy incentives
Key Insights

Social Science Insight

Lithium batteries perpetuate environmental racism and economic inequality, requiring justice-centered solutions

Design Thinking Insight

The problem is solvable through user-centered design that prioritizes both climate and community needs

Strategy Insight

Requires coordinated global governance, not just market-based solutions

Integrated Conclusion
Lithium batteries are currently neither fully sustainable nor ethical due to the human cost and environmental damage in cobalt mining, but they can become both through systematic hybrid solutions that address social justice, design innovation, and policy reform simultaneously.
